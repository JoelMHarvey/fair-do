**Body:**

Hi [First name],

If you have used other tutoring platforms before, you may have noticed something slightly confusing at checkout: the tutor's advertised rate is not the number you actually pay. That gap — sometimes 20%, sometimes 35% — is a platform service fee, added at the point of payment and not always clearly labelled.

On Tutorful, for example, a tutor who charges £40 per hour results in a parent bill of roughly £54. The tutor still receives £40. The platform keeps £14 for making the introduction. That fee continues for as long as you use that tutor, on every single lesson, indefinitely.

fair-do does not do this.

**What you see on your invoice is what your tutor charges.** We do not add a service fee. We do not take a percentage from your payments. If your tutor charges £45 per hour, your invoice says £45. That is the end of it.

This is not a promotion or an introductory offer. It is the model. We charge tutors a flat monthly subscription instead — they choose to be here because keeping 100% of what they earn is worth more to them than the commission they would lose elsewhere. In return, you get a direct financial relationship with the person teaching your child, without a platform sitting in the middle taking a cut of every lesson.

**Why does this matter to you, practically?**

It means your tutor is not quietly building a case to move you off-platform to avoid the fee. It means the rate you agreed at the start is the rate you will pay a year from now. It means when you compare two tutors, you are comparing like for like — not trying to reverse-engineer what the real cost will be after platform markup.

**How to compare tutoring platforms honestly:**

When you look at any platform, ask two questions: what does the tutor see from each lesson, and what does the parent pay? The gap between those two numbers is the platform's extraction. On fair-do, that gap is zero.

### Email 3 — How to Get the Most Out of Tutoring

**Subject:** What parents who get results actually do (it is not what you think)
**Preview:** Three practical things — none of which require you to sit in on lessons.

**Body:**

Hi [First name],

This email is not marketing. It is the most useful thing we can tell you based on what we have seen work for families navigating secondary school tutoring.

None of this requires extra money. It mostly requires about twenty minutes a week.

**1. Read the session notes — and respond to them**

If your tutor is leaving notes after each session (check the portal's Session Notes tab), the most valuable thing you can do is read them and acknowledge one thing from the last lesson when you next see your child. You do not need to quiz them or run a mini-lesson yourself. Just saying "your tutor mentioned you found integration by parts tricky — is that making more sense now?" signals to your child that sessions matter and are connected to real progress, not just a weekly ritual.

If your tutor is not yet leaving notes, it is worth asking them to start. Most are glad to — they simply do not always know whether parents are reading the portal.

**2. Set a goal at the start of each term, not the end**

The most common pattern we see is parents waiting until a bad mock result arrives before discussing goals with the tutor. By then, time is short and anxiety is high. A brief message to your tutor at the start of each term — even just "we want [child's name] to feel confident going into the Unit 3 mock in March" — gives the tutor something concrete to plan toward. It also makes it easier to evaluate whether sessions are working before a high-stakes assessment.

Your portal's messaging tab connects directly to your tutor. A two-sentence note at the start of term takes less time than any conversation you will have about exam results.

**3. Know when to step back**

For Years 10–13 especially: one of the things tutoring does well is give students a space where they can admit confusion without the social cost of doing it in a classroom. That dynamic only works if the student feels some ownership over it. Sitting in on sessions occasionally can be useful — particularly for younger students — but for most secondary pupils, knowing that sessions are between them and their tutor, with notes available to you afterwards, is the better arrangement.

The goal is not a permanent tutor. The goal is a student who does not need one. Good tutors work toward their own redundancy, and the best way to support that is to let the process run while staying informed through the notes and check-ins.

### Email 4 — Exam Season Calendar

**Subject:** Your month-by-month tutoring calendar for exam season
**Preview:** When to start, when to consolidate, when to stop adding new content. Print this and stick it up.

**Body:**

Hi [First name],

This is the email to save. Or print. Or forward to another parent who is figuring out the same thing.

The biggest mistake families make with tutoring in exam years is not starting too late — it is not knowing what to do when. A student who starts revision in January but has no structure will lose as much ground in February as they gained. What follows is the calendar we would give every Year 10, 11, 12, and 13 student if we could.

Adapt it to your child's specific exam board and subject mix. But the shape holds across almost all UK secondary curricula.

---

**SEPTEMBER – OCTOBER**
*Purpose: Foundation and diagnosis*

This is the time to identify the gaps, not fill them. A good tutor should spend the first few weeks of the year working out which areas of the specification your child is shaky on — not just the topics they say they find hard, but the ones they are overconfident about.

Action for you: Ask your tutor for a brief written assessment of where your child stands across the main topic areas. It does not need to be a formal document — a few lines in the session notes will do. This becomes your baseline.

---

**NOVEMBER**
*Purpose: Consolidating misunderstood content*

Mock exams are usually in November or December for Years 11 and 13. The six weeks before a mock are not the time to cover new ground — they are the time to make sure the content already taught is actually understood, not just recognised.

Action for you: If your child has a mock coming up, ask your tutor to shift focus to past paper practice for at least two sessions in this window.

---

**DECEMBER**
*Purpose: Rest with intention*

The Christmas holiday is not dead time. It is the best opportunity for low-pressure review that most students waste. Three or four hours per week of light revision — going through tutor notes, re-reading summaries, doing a timed paper without exam conditions — compounds significantly by January.

Action for you: Book a session in the last week of the holidays if you can. Coming back to school without having completely switched off makes the January return easier.

---

**JANUARY – FEBRUARY**
*Purpose: Closing the gaps identified in mocks*

Mock results land in January. This is the moment most parents panic and try to increase session frequency dramatically. That often backfires — students who go from one session to four sessions per week in January frequently plateau by March from fatigue.

Better: use the mock result to identify two or three specific areas, increase focus on those in existing sessions, and keep the overall volume manageable.

Action for you: Look at the mark scheme commentary, not just the grade. Where exactly did the marks go? Share that with your tutor so sessions target the actual weaknesses, not just the subjects that got a low mark.

---

**MARCH – APRIL**
*Purpose: Full specification coverage complete, timed practice begins*

By the start of April, all new content should be covered. If it is not, that is the conversation to have with your tutor now — not in May. From this point, every session should involve at least some element of timed practice under something resembling exam conditions.

Action for you: If your child does not have a quiet, timed, distraction-free place to practise at home, sort that now. The physical conditions of revision matter more than most students admit.

---

**EASTER HOLIDAY**
*Purpose: Intensive revision, spaced*

This is the highest-leverage fortnight of the year. A student who does five to six hours of focused revision per day across the Easter holiday — structured, spaced, not cramming — arrives at May with a significant advantage.

Action for you: Keep at least one tutoring session per week during the holidays. Let your child keep their social plans — complete isolation tends to create anxiety rather than reduce it. But protect the revision mornings.

---

**MAY – JUNE**
*Purpose: Exam delivery, not learning*

By the time exams begin, the learning is done. Tutoring in this window should be narrow: answering specific questions, reviewing a specific paper, calming pre-exam nerves. Adding new content in May is almost always counterproductive.

Action for you: Ask your tutor to shift into a "Q&A and confidence" mode from the week before the first paper. One session the day before a key exam — reviewing one past paper together — is often more useful than five sessions the week before.

---

**AFTER THE LAST EXAM**
*Purpose: Debrief and plan forward*

For Year 11s continuing to sixth form, or Year 13s heading to university: the summer is not too early to start thinking about what comes next. A single diagnostic session in July, once results are known, can set up the following September far more productively than starting from scratch in October.

Save this email. The calendar is the same every year — only the child's year group changes.

### Email 5 — Check-in and Next Steps

**Subject:** Two months in — how is it going?
**Preview:** A quick check-in, plus two things worth doing before the end of term.

**Body:**

Hi [First name],

You have had your portal active for about two months now, so this feels like a sensible moment to check in — not with a survey, just with a few questions worth sitting with.

**Is your child making progress?**

The honest answer to this question is rarely obvious from one lesson to the next. Progress in secondary-level tutoring tends to show up in three places: greater willingness to attempt questions your child would previously have skipped, slightly faster recall on topics they have covered with the tutor, and — the most reliable signal — reduced anxiety before tests in that subject. Grade improvements follow these, usually with a lag of one to two terms.

If you are not seeing any of those three things, it is worth a direct conversation with your tutor. Not a complaint — a check-in. "How do you feel the last few sessions have gone? Is [child's name] engaging well?" Most tutors will tell you honestly if something is not clicking, and a good tutor will welcome the question.

**Two things worth doing before the end of term:**

First, open the portal and read through the last four weeks of session notes. Not to monitor, but to remind yourself of what has been covered and what your tutor flagged for further work. If you have not looked at the notes since you signed up, you may be surprised how much is there.

Second, if you have not already booked an end-of-term session, do that now. The final session of a term is a useful place to review what has been achieved, set expectations for the holiday period, and agree on when to resume. Students who have a booked session to return to tend to do more revision during the break than those who leave it open-ended.
