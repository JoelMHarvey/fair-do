### New UK tutoring platform gives tutors 100% of what they earn — and adds nothing to the parent's bill

**fair-do launches as the only tutoring platform in the UK to charge zero commission on every lesson, zero service fee at checkout, and a single transparent subscription — directly challenging the hidden extraction model that costs UK tutors an estimated £800–£1,500 every month**

---

LONDON — fair-do, a new UK-based online tutoring platform, launched today with a model that inverts the economics of a market currently worth an estimated £6–8 billion annually. While every major incumbent takes between 25% and 40% of each lesson fee — or adds a hidden service charge at checkout — fair-do charges tutors a flat monthly subscription and takes nothing from what they earn. Parents pay exactly what the tutor charges. No markup. No service fee. No surprise at checkout.

The platform launches at a moment of significant pressure in UK education. Following the January 2025 imposition of VAT on private school fees, an estimated 37,000 pupils left independent schools, many moving to intensive private tuition as an alternative. Demand for tutors has surged while the economics for those tutors remain exploitative: a tutor charging £40 per hour, running 20 sessions a week on a 25% commission platform, loses between £3,120 and £8,320 every year compared with what they would keep on fair-do — for exactly the same work, the same students, and the same outcomes. fair-do's Free tier, which covers tutors with up to eight students, costs nothing and requires no credit card. The Pro tier, at £29 per month, covers unlimited students and advanced tools including AI post-session summaries, a parent portal, and priority support. A Schools tier at £79 per month adds B2B management tools and email-domain enrolment for organisations.

The platform has been built to compete with — and surpass — the product depth of incumbents that have had years to build features. fair-do includes an integrated video classroom, a collaborative Excalidraw whiteboard, group lesson functionality, gift vouchers, a dual-sided referral programme, a credential audit trail, a safeguarding framework, and a resource library. It is also the only UK tutoring platform with an Anthropic Claude integration in production, enabling AI-generated post-session summaries and positioning it to launch AI lesson plan generation — a feature that does not currently exist anywhere in the UK market and that could eliminate 60–90% of unpaid tutor prep time for new students. The nearest competitor comparison points are stark: Tutorful charges a 35% service fee on every parent invoice; MyTutor takes 40% from tutors and has just been acquired by a US corporate group; GoStudent raised $779 million and has already been revalued below $1 billion.

fair-do is accepting founding tutor registrations today at fair-do.co.uk. Founding tutors receive locked-in Pro pricing, priority listing, and access to the platform's early-access feature set. B2B and school enquiries are open via the School tier.

### Partnership 1: The Tutors' Association (TTA)

**Why they matter:** The TTA is the UK's professional body for private tutors. A formal relationship with them provides fair-do with instant credibility, access to their membership, and alignment with the accreditation and safeguarding standards that schools and parents look for. Their endorsement converts fair-do from "a new platform" into "a professionally recognised platform."

**What fair-do offers them:** A co-branded "TTA Verified" credential badge displayed on every qualifying tutor's fair-do profile; a free School-tier account for the TTA's own administrative use; shared data on tutor earnings and market conditions for their annual sector report; and a preferred-platform designation in their member communications.

**What fair-do wants from them:** A formal preferred-platform designation communicated to their membership; a co-authored article for their newsletter or site on tutor economics and the commission model; inclusion in their onboarding materials for new members; and a quote from their chair or director for press materials.

## SECTION 5: CRISIS COMMUNICATIONS

### Scenario 1: A Safeguarding Incident Becomes Public

**Situation:** A report emerges — via social media, a news outlet, or a parent complaint — that a safeguarding concern involving a lesson conducted through the fair-do platform was not handled appropriately, or that a tutor with a relevant history was active on the platform.

---

**Response Framework**

*Immediate (within 2 hours of incident becoming public):*

1. Take the affected tutor's profile offline immediately pending investigation. Do not communicate this publicly in the first instance.
2. Contact the affected family directly, privately, and before any public statement. This conversation takes absolute priority over press management.
3. Report to the relevant statutory authority (where appropriate: local authority designated officer / LADO, or police) before issuing any public communication.
4. Appoint a single spokesperson. No other team member speaks to press or public.

---

**Public Statement (to be issued once statutory notification is made):**

> fair-do takes safeguarding with absolute seriousness. We are aware of a concern relating to a tutor on our platform and we are cooperating fully with [relevant authority]. The tutor's profile has been suspended while an investigation is under way. We are in direct contact with the family affected and our focus is entirely on their welfare.
>
> We will not comment further on the specifics of an active investigation. Any media enquiries should be directed to press@fair-do.co.uk.

---

**Follow-up (within 48 hours, if investigation warrants):**

> We want to be transparent about what happened and what we are doing. [Factual account of what occurred.] fair-do operates a credential audit trail and a safeguarding framework that includes [specific measures]. In response to this incident we are [specific additional steps — third-party audit, enhanced DBS check protocol, independent safeguarding review]. We will publish the outcome of that review publicly.

---

**Principles for this scenario:**
- Never lead with defending the platform. Lead with the person affected.
- Do not issue a holding statement that implies the complaint may be exaggerated or unfounded.
- Proactive transparency — publishing the review outcome even if uncomfortable — is the only credible path to trust recovery.
- If media coverage is inaccurate on a factual point, correct it once, directly, and do not engage in a sustained back-and-forth.

---

### Scenario 2: A Tutor Publicly Complains About the Platform

**Situation:** A tutor posts on social media, Reddit, or a tutor forum — or contacts a journalist — alleging that fair-do has treated them unfairly: a profile was suspended without explanation, a payout was delayed, or the platform's practices do not match its marketing claims.

---

**Response Framework**

*Immediate:*

1. Read the complaint in full before responding. Identify whether it is factually accurate, partially accurate, or inaccurate.
2. Do not respond publicly with a defensive or dismissive reply within the first hour. A poor response amplifies the complaint.
3. Contact the tutor directly and privately first, regardless of whether the complaint is fair.

---

**Public Response (social media / forum — to be posted under the fair-do account):**

> Hi [Name] — thank you for raising this publicly; we would rather know than not. We have sent you a direct message and we would like to resolve this properly. If there is something we have got wrong, we will fix it and we will tell you we have fixed it.
>
> We built fair-do because platform economics are broken. We take that obligation seriously and that applies to how we treat the tutors on the platform, not just what we charge them.

---

**If the complaint is factually accurate (fair-do made an error):**

> We got this wrong. [Specific thing that went wrong.] We have corrected it. [Specific correction.] We are also reviewing our process for [relevant area] to make sure this does not happen again. [Name], please come back to us directly if anything is still unresolved.

---

**If the complaint is factually inaccurate:**

> We want to address this directly because we think there may be a misunderstanding about [specific point]. [Clear, specific, non-combative correction — one paragraph maximum.] We have reached out to [Name] privately to walk through this in detail. We are always willing to be held to account and if we have not explained our policies clearly enough, that is also something we need to fix.

---

**Principles for this scenario:**
- Never match the emotional register of an angry post. Warm, specific, and factual outperforms defensive every time.
- Do not delete or hide the complaint unless it contains private information that needs redacting.
- If the complaint is amplified by a journalist, offer the journalist access to the resolution as well as the complaint.
- A tutor who feels heard and resolved often becomes an advocate. A tutor who feels dismissed writes a second post.

---

### Scenario 3: A Competitor Claims fair-do Is Not Actually Free

**Situation:** A competitor, an anonymous post, or a journalist claim that fair-do's "0% commission" is misleading — for example, that the subscription fee is a hidden cost equivalent to commission, that the free tier has undisclosed limitations, or that the model is unsustainable and tutors will eventually face charges.

---

**Response Framework**

*Immediate:*

1. Establish which specific claim is being made. "Not actually free" covers several distinct allegations, each requiring a different factual response.
2. Do not respond to a competitor-originated claim by naming the competitor. Respond to the claim on its merits only.
3. Publish a clear, permanent "How our pricing works" page at a stable URL that you can link to from any response.

---

**Public Response (social / press):**

> Let us be specific, because the detail matters.
>
> fair-do's Free tier has no monthly fee, no commission, no credit card requirement, and no trial period. It covers up to eight students indefinitely. There is no hidden charge.
>
> Our Pro tier costs £29 per month and covers unlimited students. A tutor with nine regular students on a 25% commission platform doing four lessons a week pays approximately £360 in commission every month. Our subscription costs £29. The comparison is not close.
>
> We add nothing to the parent's invoice at checkout. What the tutor charges is what the parent pays. This is verifiable at fair-do.co.uk/pricing — every tier, every figure, stated plainly.
>
> If anyone has a specific factual concern about how our pricing works, we will answer it directly. That is the whole point.

---

**If challenged specifically on "the subscription IS a commission":**

> Commission is a percentage taken from each transaction. It rises as the tutor earns more, falls when they earn less, and is invisible to the parent. Our subscription is a fixed monthly fee that is the same whether a tutor earns £200 that month or £2,000. A tutor who moves their twelve existing students from a 25% commission platform to fair-do's Pro tier saves approximately £400 a month. These are not equivalent models.

---

**If a journalist pursues the "sustainability" angle ("how long can you offer this?"):**

> Our model is sustainable because it is simple: tutors pay a predictable subscription, we run the platform, everyone knows what the deal is. The platforms charging 30–40% commission are sustainable too — they are just sustainable at the tutor's expense. We have chosen a different trade-off. We make less per transaction and more over time, because tutors who keep their earnings stay on the platform.

---

**Principles for this scenario:**
- Specificity is the only credible defence against a vagueness attack. Respond with exact numbers, exact tier descriptions, and exact comparisons.
- Keep the stable pricing URL in every public response. Make the verification one click away.
- Do not accuse a named competitor of originating the claim unless you can prove it and are prepared for a legal response.
- If the claim spreads widely, consider a short-form video from the founder walking through the pricing page in real time. Showing is more persuasive than asserting.
