## Always-On Content Pillars

*Six recurring formats to be scheduled monthly, rotating across all channels.*

---

### Pillar 1 — "The Commission Counter"

**Format:** Static image or X/LinkedIn post with a single earnings calculation.
**Cadence:** Once per month, different tutor scenario each time.
**Example:** "A specialist French tutor. £55/hour. 15 students. Charges 10 months a year. Here's the difference between a commission platform and fair-do in a single number: £5,940."
**Why it works:** Specific numbers are shareable and credible. Every scenario resonates with a different segment of the tutor audience. No argument needed — the maths speaks.

## Engagement Playbook

*Five rules for responding to comments and DMs across all channels.*

---

### Rule 1 — Respond to every tutor complaint about commission platforms

**How:** When a tutor comments about their own commission experience (on any post, on any account), respond quickly (within 2 hours where possible), acknowledge the specific number or grievance they've named, and give them one concrete reason to look at fair-do. Do not be promotional first. Be human first.

**Example response:** "That's exactly the calculation we kept hearing. £X/hour × 20 sessions × 30% is real money leaving your account every week for no defensible reason. The founding tier is open if you want to see how the numbers compare for your rate — happy to run it for you."

**Rule for DMs:** If a tutor DMs with a complaint or a question about commission, respond with a personalised earnings calculation for their stated rate and hours. This is the single most effective conversion tool and it costs nothing.

---

### Rule 2 — Never respond to competitor mentions aggressively

**How:** If a comment mentions Tutorful, MyTutor, or another platform (either defending them or criticising them), do not attack. Acknowledge the point factually, then pivot to fair-do's model. The tone is "we made different choices for these reasons" — not "they're wrong."

**Example response to a Tutorful defender:** "Tutorful has a strong marketplace and good UX — we'd agree on that. The structural difference is what happens at checkout: their 35% service fee is on the student's bill, not the tutor's rate. On fair-do, the tutor's rate is the parent's bill. Different choices. Happy to show the checkout comparison if it's useful."

**Why this matters:** Aggressive competitor posts create legal risk, alienate undecided tutors, and undermine the brand's "warm but direct" personality. The numbers make the case — the brand doesn't need to.

---

### Rule 3 — Treat parent comments as press opportunities

**How:** Parents commenting (especially if they're sharing their own checkout fee surprise or asking about finding a tutor) should receive a response that is warm, specific, and informative — and can be quoted. The fair-do brand voice with parents is a knowledgeable friend explaining the market honestly, not a sales team.

**Example response:** "The checkout fee is genuinely one of the industry's worst-kept secrets — tutors often don't know it's there either. On fair-do, what your tutor charges is what you pay at checkout. If you're looking for a tutor in [subject], happy to help you find someone on the platform."

**Platform note:** On TikTok and Instagram, parent comments often appear under posts targeted at tutors — treat them as a discovery moment. A good reply to a parent comment can outperform the original post in reach.

---

### Rule 4 — Use X replies to build press and policy relationships

**How:** When journalists (TES, Guardian Education, Schools Week, Which?, MoneyWeek), policy accounts, teacher unions, or education commentators post about tutoring economics, private school VAT changes, tutor pay, or edtech — reply with a relevant fact or data point from the fair-do model. Not an advertisement. A contribution to the conversation.

**Example:** If a journalist tweets about the post-VAT tutoring demand surge, reply: "The demand shift is real — we're seeing it in sign-ups. The interesting structural question is where the money goes: most of that new tutoring spend flows through commission platforms taking 25–40%. The supply-side economics for tutors are still broken even when demand is strong."

**Follow up:** If the reply gets engagement, DM the journalist with a press pack or offer an interview. The X reply is the door. The DM is the pitch.

---

### Rule 5 — Create a closing ritual for all active DM conversations

**How:** Every DM conversation — whether from a tutor asking about the platform, a parent looking for a tutor, or a journalist asking for data — should end with a clear next step and a named contact. Never let a DM conversation close on "let me know if you have questions." Close on: "Here's the link / here's the number / here's the person to speak to."

**For tutors:** Close with the founding tutor sign-up link and a personalised earnings calculation if you have their rate.
**For parents:** Close with a link to browse tutors by subject + postcode, and a reassurance about the 0% checkout fee.
**For press:** Close with a press pack link and a named email address for follow-up.
**For schools/B2B:** Close with a link to the School tier page and an offer to arrange a 20-minute call.

**Why this matters:** The platform is early-stage and every conversion is manual until organic growth takes over. A DM that ends without a next step is a lost founding tutor or a lost press relationship. The engagement playbook is also the growth strategy.
