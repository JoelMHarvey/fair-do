## The vision (the crux of everything)

We built fair-do because we think tutors — people who trained for years, who know their subject, who genuinely change outcomes for families — should keep what they earn. We think quality tutoring should reach more families, not fewer, and that the only thing standing in the way is a pricing model that extracts 25–40% from every lesson for no good reason. We charge a flat monthly subscription to keep the lights on. That's it. No per-lesson fees, no commission, no cut.

**Three pillars:**

1. **Fair pay** — tutors keep 100% of every lesson; we never take a percentage of any lesson
2. **Access** — transparent pricing means more families can afford consistent tutoring, not just occasional panic sessions before exams
3. **No extraction** — the platform makes money from subscriptions only; we take no commission on any lesson, ever

**Tone:** warm, direct, ambitious. Never clinical, never salesy. We don't bash competitors — we just do it differently.

## Messaging hierarchy

### Level 1 — Belief (what we stand for)
Tutors are skilled professionals. They should be paid like it. A platform that takes 30% of a tutor's income doesn't work *with* tutors — it works *on* them. We built the alternative.

### Level 2 — Human benefit (what this means for real people)
For tutors: the same 20-hour week earns you £200–£400 more each month. Your clients are yours — and you keep 100% of what you earn from them. Your schedule is yours. You get professional tools without paying professional commission.

For parents and students: you pay what the tutor charges. No service fees added at checkout. No opaque pricing. The tutor you chose gets paid fairly, so they stay, they care, and they show up.

### Level 3 — Proof (the numbers that back the claim)
- Tutors keep 100% of what they earn on fair-do — no commission, ever. The UK average on competing platforms: 60–75% kept after their 25–40% cut.
- A tutor charging £40/hour, 20 sessions a week, earns £640/week on a 20%-commission platform. On fair-do, every session is the full £800. Difference: £3,120–£8,320/year — for the same work.
- Competitors charge parents an additional 35% service fee on top of the tutor's rate. fair-do doesn't.
- Free tier for up to 8 students, 0% commission on everything. No credit card required to start.

### Level 4 — CTA (what to do next)
**Tutors:** Set up your profile in under 10 minutes. Your first 8 students are free.
**Parents:** Ask your tutor if they're on fair-do — or find one who already is.

## Audience 1 — Parents & Students

### Who they are
Primarily parents of secondary school pupils (Years 9–13), concentrated in the months before mock exams (November, January) and the main exam season (May–June). Secondary audience: adult learners seeking professional or language skills, and parents of primary-age children preparing for 11+ entrance exams. Geographically spread across England and Wales, with higher concentration and higher average spend in London, the South East, and other high-cost urban areas.

They are not looking for the cheapest option — they are looking for the *right* option they can feel confident about. The emotional driver is anxiety reduction as much as academic improvement.

### Channels
- **Google Search** — "online maths tutor GCSE", "best tutoring platform UK", "find a tutor near me" — high intent, high conversion
- **Facebook and Instagram** — parent community groups (local area groups, school-year Facebook groups); carousel ads work well for "how tutoring works" explainers
- **Mumsnet and The Student Room** — organic discussion threads; a genuine presence in these communities outperforms paid advertising significantly
- **TikTok** — younger parents and sixth-form students researching platforms themselves; short "did you know?" format content on tutoring costs performs well
- **Local Facebook Groups** — "Guildford Parents", "Sheffield Mums" etc. — highly targeted, free, and trusted in a way that paid ads are not
- **YouTube** — pre-roll on revision / study skills content; also organic "how to find a good tutor" content

### Hooks

1. **The transparency hook:** "Most tutoring platforms add a 35% fee on top of the tutor's rate — at checkout, after you've already chosen someone. fair-do doesn't. What you see is what you pay."

2. **The accountability hook:** "You can see lesson notes, attendance records, and invoices in one place. Not because we push tutors to send them — because the platform makes it automatic." *(Parent Portal)*

3. **The access hook:** "Private tutoring used to mean paying platform fees on top of tutor fees. We removed the platform fee from your side of the equation. Good tutoring is expensive enough already."

4. **The VAT / private school displacement hook:** "If you moved your child out of private school after the January 2025 fee rise, intensive tutoring in core subjects is what most families are doing instead. fair-do makes that affordable for the tutor and transparent for you."

5. **The continuity hook:** "Your child's tutor keeps all session notes, attendance, and progress in one place. If they ever need to hand over to another tutor, nothing is lost. Everything is documented."

## Audience 2 — Tutors

### Who they are
Three primary profiles:

- **The experienced platform tutor** (Rachel): 3–10 years teaching or tutoring experience, already listed on Tutorful or MyTutor, increasingly resentful of commission, has an established client base that feels locked in. Has 20+ regular students and is losing £800–£1,500/month to platform commission. High conversion value; high referral potential.
- **The newly qualified teacher** (Jamie): NQT or within first 3 years of teaching, taking 1–4 private students on the side, motivated by extra income and the creative freedom of 1-to-1 teaching. Needs invoicing and a professional profile before anything else. Free tier is the entire product for them right now.
- **The specialist or career-changer**: Former professional (lawyer, engineer, NHS clinician) tutoring in their subject area at A-level or university level. Charges £50–£80/hour. Has never used a tutoring platform; self-sources clients via LinkedIn and word of mouth. Needs a credible public profile and payment infrastructure, not acquisition.

- **Direct outreach to Tutorful/MyTutor listings** — tutors with public profiles can be contacted; a message saying "keep 100% instead of losing 30% to the platform" is genuinely competitive
- **The Tutors' Association (TTA)** — membership body; sponsorship, newsletter placement, and event presence build legitimacy quickly

## Magazine / blog article (~700 words, ready to adapt)

### The hidden maths of online tutoring — and why we rebuilt it

Every week, thousands of UK tutors log onto a platform, deliver an hour of careful, skilled teaching, and then hand 25 to 40 pence of every pound they earned straight back to the platform. Not for finding the student — that happened months ago. Not for the lesson room, which cost pennies to run. Just because the contract says so.

This is the tutoring industry's politely ignored open secret.

The numbers are stark. A secondary maths tutor charging £45 per hour, working 20 sessions a week, earns £900 on paper. After a 25% platform commission, they take home £675. After 40%: £540. The range between those two figures — £135 a week, £5,400 a year — represents real money. It represents the difference between a viable independent career and something that feels more like being a contractor for a company you didn't choose to work for.

The tutors bearing this cost are not inexperienced. Many have qualified teacher status. Many have spent years building subject knowledge and a reputation for results. They chose independent tutoring precisely because they wanted professional autonomy — and they've found a form of it, up to a point.

The irony is that the tutoring market in the UK has never been larger. Approximately 30% of UK pupils have now received private tutoring, up from 18% in 2005. The market is worth an estimated £2–4 billion annually and is growing at nearly 10% per year. The shift of nearly 37,000 pupils out of private schools following the January 2025 VAT change has created a surge in demand for intensive independent tutoring that is still working through the system. The end of the National Tutoring Programme in 2024 removed state-subsidised catch-up provision and pushed that demand to the private market.

More families need tutors. More teachers want to tutor. The structural problem is the platforms sitting in the middle, taking a third of the transaction.

We built fair-do because we couldn't find a good reason for that number.

The infrastructure a tutoring platform provides — a booking system, a video room, a payment processor, a client-facing profile page — is not expensive to build or maintain. It certainly isn't worth 30% of a tutor's income, indefinitely, on every session, for students the tutor sourced themselves and has retained for years through their own quality of work.

So we priced it differently. Tutors on fair-do keep 100% of what they earn from every lesson. We charge a flat monthly subscription — free for up to 8 students, £29 per month (Pro) for unlimited students and advanced tools. No commission. No service fee added to the parent's invoice at checkout. No percentage that climbs as the tutor's reputation grows.

For parents, this has a direct effect. When tutors earn fairly, they can charge sustainably. They don't need to inflate their rates to compensate for the cut taken before they see a penny. The economics of fair tutoring and affordable tutoring are not in tension — they are the same thing.

We also think the transparency matters as much as the price. The families we've spoken to — parents trying to navigate GCSE and A-level pressure, adults returning to learning, students preparing for university — don't want opacity. They want to know what a session costs, what was covered, whether their child attended. They want invoices they can download. They want to feel like they're in a professional relationship, not a grey-market arrangement managed across three WhatsApp threads.

fair-do is early. We are building the platform we wished existed when we watched tutors — talented, qualified, experienced tutors — hand over a third of their earnings for tools worth a fraction of that. The market is large, the demand is real, and the tutors are ready for something that treats them like professionals rather than suppliers.

If you tutor, your first 8 students are free. Build your profile, set your rate, and keep what you earn.

If you're a parent looking for a tutor, start at fair-do.com. The price you see is the price the tutor sets. No fees added at checkout.

That's the whole model. We think it's enough.

## Contact lists — how to build them (not fabricated)

### Finding tutors

**LinkedIn**
Search: "private tutor" + UK location filters (London, Manchester, Birmingham, Leeds, Edinburgh, Bristol, etc.). Filter by "self-employed" or "freelance." Secondary search: "tutoring" + "self-employed" + subject area ("GCSE maths tutor", "A-level English tutor"). Join LinkedIn groups: "UK Private Tutors", "Teaching and Tutoring Network UK". Do not cold-pitch immediately — engage with posts for two weeks first, then DM with a short, honest note about the platform.

**Facebook Groups**
Search on Facebook for: "UK Private Tutors", "Online Tutors UK", "Tutoring UK Network", "Secondary School Tutors", "Primary Tutors UK", "GCSE Tutors UK". Request to join; introduce fair-do honestly in your first post. Avoid over-posting; one post per week, responding to every comment, builds more trust than broadcast advertising.

**TES Community (tes.com)**
Create a TES account and engage with the "Tutoring" and "Private Tutoring" forum sections. Tutors on TES are often teachers considering going independent — the fair-do value proposition lands well here. TES also allows advertising in their job/resource marketplace.

**Tutorful and Superprof public listings**
Both platforms display tutor profiles publicly. These can be browsed by subject and location to identify active, experienced tutors. Outreach should be via platforms that allow messaging (LinkedIn, email where listed) rather than through competitor platforms' messaging systems. Keep outreach honest: "We built an alternative to commission-heavy platforms. Here's how we're different."

**The Tutors' Association (TTA) — thetutorsassociation.org.uk**
Membership body for professional tutors. A TTA partnership or sponsorship puts fair-do in front of their membership directly. They publish a member newsletter and run regional events. Approach as a partner ("we want to support professional tutors") not a vendor.

## Launch sequence (first 6 weeks)

The sequencing principle: **tutor supply before client demand.** A parent arriving at fair-do.com with no tutors to choose from churns immediately and tells people it's empty. A tutor arriving with 50 tutors to compare themselves against signs up. Get supply first.

---

### Week 1 — Seed the tutor base (target: 30–50 tutor signups)

**Objectives:** First tutors on platform. Free tier only. Zero public advertising.

- Identify 100 tutors via LinkedIn and Facebook groups; personalised outreach to each (not a mass mail — a genuine message acknowledging their subject and experience)
- Post in 3–4 Facebook tutor groups: "We built something for tutors who are tired of 30% commission. Here's what it looks like. First 8 students are free, always." Include a short video (Script 4 — "Why we built it")
- Onboard every early tutor personally — a brief call or Loom video showing how the profile setup works; collect their feedback immediately
- Fix anything broken that early tutors surface. Do not move to Week 2 until the onboarding experience is clean.

---

### Week 2 — Profile quality and tutor content (target: 50–80 tutors, first tutor-generated sessions)

**Objectives:** Tutors complete profiles. First real sessions happen. Collect testimonials.

- Work with 5–10 early tutors to build showcase profiles — complete, photographed, compelling subject descriptions, verified experience. These are the profiles you will show in any marketing material.
- Ask every tutor who completes a session in Week 2 for a short testimonial (one sentence is enough): "I made £X this week and kept £Y." Specific numbers, with permission to use them.
- Post on LinkedIn: a short piece titled "We launched a tutoring platform last week. Here's what we learned from the first 50 tutors." Honest, specific, no puffery. Engagement from tutors who weren't in the first wave is a Week 3 acquisition mechanism.
- Begin building the press contact spreadsheet (see Contact Lists). No pitches yet — just prep.

---

### Week 3 — Content seeding and organic discovery (target: 100 tutors, first organic parent signups)

**Objectives:** Content engine running. First parents arriving organically.

- Publish the magazine article ("The hidden maths of online tutoring") on the fair-do blog. Optimise for search: target keywords "tutoring platform UK", "tutoring commission", "how much do tutoring platforms take". Submit to Google Search Console.
- Post Script 1 ("The maths") video to TikTok, Instagram Reels, and LinkedIn. Do not boost with paid budget yet — test organic first.
- Share the blog article in the Facebook tutor groups where you posted in Week 1. Frame it as useful information for tutors deciding where to list, not as marketing.
- Pitch the article to one education publication (TES first; if declined, Schools Week). Offer it as an exclusive for 2 weeks, then publish on the blog regardless.
- First parent portal: any tutors with active students on the platform should be invited to enable the Parent Portal for their students' parents. Collect feedback on what parents say they want from it.

---

### Week 4 — First paid experiment and Mumsnet (target: 130 tutors, 20–30 parent portal activations)

**Objectives:** Test paid acquisition on the parent side. Build credibility in parent communities.

- Allocate a small budget (£200–£400) to Google Search ads targeting: "online maths tutor GCSE", "online English tutor A-level", "tutoring platform UK". Measure cost per parent signup carefully. The goal is data, not scale.
- Post a genuine, non-promotional piece in a Mumsnet Talk thread or create a Mumsnet Bloggers post: "What I learned comparing tutoring platforms as a parent — what the fees actually look like." This needs to be honest and useful, not an advert. Mumsnet communities are highly sceptical of promotional content and very receptive to genuine consumer advocacy.
- Send first email newsletter to the tutor list: subject line "What 100 tutors told us in week one." Share real feedback, what you've fixed, what's coming. Build the relationship with the tutor base explicitly — they are not customers to market at, they are partners whose trust you are earning.
- Follow up with any tutors who started signup but didn't complete profile — a personal nudge (not an automated drip) converts better at this stage.

---

### Week 5 — Referral mechanics and exam season positioning (target: 180 tutors, first press coverage)

**Objectives:** Tutors become the acquisition channel for parents. First external press mention.

- Launch tutor referral: any tutor who invites another tutor and that tutor completes 5 sessions gets one month of Pro free. Simple, trackable, no complicated tier structure.
- Pitch the article to The Guardian Education desk and to Which? simultaneously (they are not in the same competitive set — both pitches can run). Angle for Guardian: "The tutoring platform inequality no one talks about." Angle for Which?: "Tutoring platform fees compared: what families are really paying."
- Post Script 2 ("For tutors") on TikTok and Instagram. Boost this one with £100 of paid spend to tutor audiences on Meta (target: "self-employed", "education", "teacher", UK). Track cost per tutor signup.
- Create one shareable asset for tutors to send to their clients: a one-page PDF or landing page card that explains the Parent Portal. Make it easy for tutors to introduce their clients to fair-do — the ask is small ("here's where you can see your invoices and lesson notes") and the value is immediate.

---

### Week 6 — Review, consolidate, and plan Month 2

**Objectives:** Data review. Identify what's working. Lock in Month 2 plan.

- Pull all metrics: tutor signups, profile completion rate, sessions held, parent portal activations, paid ad cost per signup, organic search impressions, content engagement.
- Interview 5 tutors (15 minutes each): what made them sign up, what almost stopped them, what they'd tell another tutor considering fair-do. Transcribe these — they are your copywriting resource for Month 2.
- Interview 5 parents: what they use the portal for, what they wish it did, whether they've recommended fair-do to another parent.
- Publish a transparent "week 6 update" on the blog and on LinkedIn: how many tutors, how many sessions, what you've learned. Transparency about being early builds trust faster than polish does.
- Decide Month 2 priorities: if tutor growth is strong and parent growth is lagging, double down on parent acquisition (Google Ads, Mumsnet, parent-side content). If tutors are plateauing, assess whether the referral mechanic is working or whether outreach channels need to expand (Reddit, TTA partnership, regional Facebook groups).

---

**The north star metric for the first 6 weeks:** tutors who complete a profile, hold at least one session, and return the following week. Everything else — signups, traffic, press mentions — is a leading indicator of that number. A tutor who has held one session on fair-do and found it simpler and more profitable than their previous platform will not leave. That is the product's real test.
